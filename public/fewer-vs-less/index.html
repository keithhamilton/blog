Fewer vs. Less

13 April 2015

I have less apples than I did yesterday.

I've been reading a lot of essays on the "correctness" of Standard English lately, and I've been waffling on the idea of it being correct or not. In some regards, it seems perfectly correct that there is a standard set of grammar rules, and that they can be violated. In other regards, one must ask, "How do those rules get made in the first place? Why is one thing correct in the first place?"

Take "fewer" versus "less." If we go by Standard English, this is a mistake you will hear and/or make every day. The rule goes like this:

For a collection of things, if the "thing" can be counted, use "fewer," otherwise use "less."

So, for example, you would never say "I have 15 clothing," because "clothing" is not an enumerable (countable) thing. It follows that if you got rid of some clothing, you would say "I have less clothing than I used to have."

Might you ever say "I have 25 apples"? Yes, you might say that. If you ate three apples, you would then say "I have fewer apples than I used to have."

This is the "correct" way to say something is in a smaller quantity than something else. My question now is "why?" A strong defense for there being no "correct" English is that there really is no logical reason why this rule exists; fewer is specifically a comparator for numbers, whereas less has a broader scope, yet includes numeric amount. It is likely the "fewer vs less" rule was just a stylistic convention at one point and became "law."

It's obvious that just using "less" all of the time wouldn't conflate meaning; if it did, no one would make the mistake of saying "less" in place of "fewer" in general. You would never say "I have smaller apples" just after eating a few, because "smaller" is clearly a size comparator, not a quantity comparator. "Less" and "fewer," however, are both quantity compartors, fewer just being a little more limited in what it can describe.

Some will argue that because something is said in general it is inherently correct. I suppose I can take that as true, as all artistic convention is just that—the majority opinion and execution of its practictioners. At any rate, the question of whether there is a "correct" English demands a very important question be answered:

How do rules of grammar get made in the first place?

I don't mean "what authority" or "what group," I mean whatever body decides what the rules are...where does it get its information? It seems preposterous that a group of people would sit around in a room and just fabricate rules based on fancy. What seems more reasonable is that the group of people who "decide" what standard language is are taking general usage and trying to fabricate rules that describe it.

If "general usage" is the north star for the group of grammarians who codify our language, then we, the entire body of speakers, are the ones who make the rules. If this is true, then there really is only the language as spoken, and its late-to-the-party counterpart, grammar.

Ok, so...is it incorrect to say "I have less apples than I had yesterday"?

Yes. For now.
